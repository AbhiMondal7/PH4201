\section{Introduction}
The Faraday effect, also known as Faraday rotation, is a magneto-optic phenomenon that demonstrates the fundamental interaction between light and a magnetic field. Discovered by Michael Faraday, this effect reveals that isotropic media become optically active when a magnetic field is applied parallel to the propagation direction of the light. This pivotal observation served as one of the earliest indications of the deep, underlying connection between optics and electromagnetism. Specifically, introducing a magnetic field in the direction of a light beam's propagation through a transparent medium induces a phenomenon known as circular birefringence.

As linearly polarized light travels through the optical medium under these conditions, its plane of polarization undergoes a measurable rotation. The angle of this rotation is a linear function of both the mean magnetic flux density and the length of the optical medium. The factor of proportionality governing this relationship is known as Verdet's constant. This specific experiment focuses on measuring and interpreting this magnetically induced circular birefringence.

\section{Theory}

\subsection{Optical Rotation for Linearly Polarized Light}

A linearly polarized plane wave propagating along the $x$-direction can be expressed as a superposition of right and left circularly polarized components:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{E}(x,t) = \mathbf{E}_r + \mathbf{E}_l
\end{equation}
where 
\begin{subequations}
    \begin{align}
        E_r(x,t) &= \left\{ 0,\; \frac{A}{2}\cos\left[\omega\left(t - \frac{n_r x}{c}\right)\right],\; \frac{A}{2}\sin\left[\omega\left(t - \frac{n_r x}{c}\right)\right] \right\} \label{eq:Er}\\
        E_l(x,t) &= \left\{ 0,\; \frac{A}{2}\cos\left[\omega\left(t - \frac{n_l x}{c}\right)\right],\; -\frac{A}{2}\sin\left[\omega\left(t - \frac{n_l x}{c}\right)\right] \right\} \label{eq:El}
    \end{align}
\end{subequations}
represent the electric vectors in plane waves of right and left circularly polarized light,
respectively.  The amplitude of $E$ is $A$, and the velocity of propagation is $c/n$ where $n$ is the index of refraction. Thus a beam of linearly polarized light can be considered as a linear superposition of right and left circularly polarized components.
\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width = 0.8\textwidth]{Image/Electromagnetic wave.png}
    \caption{Addition of right and left circularly polarized components on two planes defined by $x=0$ and $x=D$, respectively. Because $n_l > n_r$, $E_l$ is more retarded in phase at $x=D$ relative to $x=0$ than $E_r$, with the result that their sum at $x=D$ is rotated clockwise by the angle $\psi = \pi (n_l - n_r)D/\lambda$.}
    \label{fig:lin_to_circ}
\end{figure}
To indicate a difference in velocities for right and left circularly polarized waves the index n is replaced in equations (\ref{eq:Er}) and (\ref{eq:El}) by $n_r$ and $n_l$, respectively. As illustrated in \cref{fig:lin_to_circ}, the electric vector on the y-z plane at x=D is then
\begin{equation}
\boldsymbol{E}(D,t) = \boldsymbol{E}_r(D,t) + \boldsymbol{E}_l(D,t)
\end{equation}
Using the identities
\begin{equation}
\cos\alpha + \cos\beta = 2 \cos\left(\frac{\alpha+\beta}{2}\right)\cos\left(\frac{\alpha-\beta}{2}\right)
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}
\sin\alpha - \sin\beta = 2 \cos\left(\frac{\alpha+\beta}{2}\right)\sin\left(\frac{\alpha-\beta}{2}\right)
\end{equation}
we find for the electric field at $x = D$ the expression
\begin{equation*}
\begin{aligned}\boldsymbol{E}(D,t) = \{
&0, \; A \cos\left[\omega\left(t - (n_r + n_l)D/2c\right)\right] \cos\left[\omega(n_l - n_r)D/2c\right], \\ 
&A \cos\left[\omega\left(t - (n_r + n_l)D/2c\right)\right] \sin\left[\omega(n_l - n_r)D/2c\right] \}
\end{aligned}
\end{equation*}
This represents an oscillating electric field of amplitude A inclined with respect to the y-
axis by an angle

\begin{equation}
\psi = \tan^{-1}\left( \frac{E_z}{E_y} \right) = \frac{\omega (n_l - n_r)D}{2c} = \frac{\pi (n_l - n_r)D}{\lambda}, \label{eq:optical_rot}
\end{equation}
where $\lambda$ is the wavelength of the light in vacuum. Thus circular birefringence can be explained as an effect of a difference in the propagation velocities of right and left circularly polarized light. The difference is generally very small, amounting to only a few parts per million in optically active materials as well as in the Faraday effect at moderate magnetic field strengths. However, in a typical setup $D/\lambda \gg 1$ so that even minute differences between $n_l$ and $n_r$ yield values of $\psi$ which are large enough to be measured accurately.

\begin{equation}
\boxed{\psi = \frac{\pi D}{\lambda}(n_l - n_r)}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Becquerel calculation on frequency shift}
We consider a transparent medium composed of particles of mass $m$ and charge $q$. These particles are embedded in a background of opposite charge and are bound by an elastic restoring force, allowing them to oscillate about fixed equilibrium positions.

We examine the case where a constant magnetic field $\mathbf{B}$ of magnitude $B$ is applied along the $+x$-direction. A plane right circularly polarized electromagnetic wave propagates along the same direction. At a given point, the wave produces a time-varying electric field $\mathbf{E}$ of constant magnitude $E$, which rotates in a plane perpendicular to $\mathbf{B}$ and in a clockwise sense relative to the direction of $\mathbf{B}$.
\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width = 0.6\textwidth]{Image/Becquerel_img.jpg}
    \caption{A particle of mass $m$ and charge $q$ is attached to a spring with spring constant $k$, which is fixed along the $x$-axis. The direction of propagation of light and the applied magnetic field $\mathbf{B}$ are both along the $x$-axis. The electric field $\mathbf{E}$ is perpendicular to the $x$-axis and rotates in the $yz$-plane.}
    \label{fig:Becquerel_img}
\end{figure}

Under \emph{steady-state conditions}, the charged particles execute circular motion of radius $r$, governed by the equation (in cgs units):
\begin{equation}
- m \omega^2 r = - k r + qE + \frac{qB}{c}\,\omega r
\end{equation}

where $k$ represents the effective spring constant associated with the restoring force. For left circularly polarized light, the sign of the magnetic term changes.

Solving the above equation for $r$, we obtain
\begin{equation}
r = \frac{qE/m}{\omega_0^2 - \omega^2 - \frac{qB}{mc}\,\omega}
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation}
\omega_0 = \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}}.
\end{equation}

A displacement of a charge $q$ from its oppositely charged equilibrium position by a distance $r$ produces an electric dipole of magnitude $qr$. If there are $N$ such dipoles per unit volume, the polarization is given by $P = Nqr$, and the permittivity becomes
\[
\varepsilon = 1 + \frac{4\pi P}{E}.
\]

Thus, for a dielectric medium with permeability $\mu = 1$, the velocity of propagation is $v = \frac{c}{\sqrt{\varepsilon}}$. In the presence of a magnetic field, the refractive index for right circularly polarized light is

\begin{equation}
n_r(\omega) = \frac{c}{v} = \left[ 1 + \frac{4\pi N q^2 / m}{\omega_0^2 - \omega^2 - \frac{qB}{mc}\,\omega} \right]^{1/2}
\end{equation}
and for left circularly polarized light,
\begin{equation}
n_l(\omega) = \frac{c}{v} = \left[ 1 + \frac{4\pi N q^2 / m}{\omega_0^2 - \omega^2 + \frac{qB}{mc}\,\omega} \right]^{1/2}.
\end{equation}
Above equations indicate that the refractive indices diverge as the frequency of light approaches the condition
\[
\omega_0^2 - \omega^2 \pm \frac{qB}{mc}\,\omega = 0.
\]
This unphysical behaviour arises from the absence of a damping term in the equation of motion. In a more realistic treatment, a damping force proportional to the particle velocity is included, which accounts for absorption. Both the refractive index and the absorption coefficient exhibit maxima near $\omega_0$. However, for frequencies sufficiently far from $\omega_0$, neglecting damping introduces negligible error.

The influence of the magnetic field on the refractive indices of circularly polarized light depends on the sign of the product $qB$. For right circularly polarized light with the magnetic field directed along the propagation direction ($B_x > 0$) and for negatively charged particles ($q < 0$), the product $qB < 0$. This leads to a decrease in $n_r$ and a corresponding increase in $n_l$. Hence, $(n_l - n_r) > 0$, which implies a positive rotation angle.

The change in $n_r$ due to the applied magnetic field can be interpreted as an effective shift in the frequency from $\omega$ to $\omega + \Delta \omega_r$, where $\Delta \omega_r$ satisfies
\begin{equation}
\omega^2 + \frac{qB}{mc}\,\omega = (\omega + \Delta \omega_r)^2.
\end{equation}

Solving this equation and neglecting higher-order small terms gives
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\Delta \omega_r = \frac{qB}{2mc}.}
\end{equation}

Similarly, for left circularly polarized light,
\begin{equation}
\omega^2 - \frac{qB}{mc}\,\omega = (\omega + \Delta \omega_l)^2,
\end{equation}

which yields
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\Delta \omega_l = -\frac{qB}{2mc}.}
\end{equation}

Since these frequency shifts are much smaller than $\omega$, the difference in refractive indices can be expressed to a very good approximation using a \emph{first-order expansion.}
\begin{equation}
n_l - n_r = \frac{dn}{d\omega}(\Delta \omega_l - \Delta \omega_r).
\end{equation}
Substituting the expressions for $\Delta \omega_r$ and $\Delta \omega_l$, we obtain
\begin{equation}
n_l - n_r = \frac{dn}{d\lambda}\left(\frac{\lambda^2}{2\pi c}\right)\left(\frac{qB}{mc}\right), \label{eq:refrac_diff}
\end{equation}
where $\lambda = \frac{2\pi c}{\omega}$ is the wavelength of light in vacuum.

Combining \cref{eq:optical_rot} and \ref{eq:refrac_diff} the \emph{Faraday rotation} angle is given by
\begin{equation}
\psi = \frac{dn}{d\lambda} \frac{\lambda}{2c^2}\frac{q}{m} \, DB.
\end{equation}
Experimentally it was verified that the Faraday rotation angle $\psi$ is proportional to the product of $D$ and the magnetic field $B$, thus
\begin{equation}
    \boxed{\psi = VDB,}
\end{equation}
Thus, the \textbf{Verdet constant} can be written as by comparing 
\begin{equation}
\boxed{V = \frac{dn}{d\lambda} \frac{\lambda}{2c^2}\frac{q}{m}.}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
\Psi = VLH, 
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
V = \frac{\Psi}{L \cdot H} = \frac{\text{slope}}{\text{L}}. 
\end{equation}

From the slope of the $\Psi$ vs $H$ plot,
\[
V = \frac{0.196 \pm 0.004}{10} = 0.0196 \pm 0.004 \text{ min Oe}^{-1} \text{ cm}^{-1},

\subsection{Verification for Malus's Law}
The plot of intensity versus current shows a non-linear increase in intensity with increasing current. Since the current through the electromagnet is directly proportional to the magnetic field, and hence to the rotation angle $\theta$, this effectively represents the variation of intensity with polarization angle. According to Malus's law,
\[
I = I_{0}cos^{2}\theta
\]
the transmitted intensity should vary as a cosine-squared function of the angle between the P1 and P2. In the present setup, the P2 is initially oriented perpendicular to the plane of polarization. Due to this, an additional phase shift of $\frac{\pi}{2}$ is introduced, and the intensity follows a $\sin^2\theta$ dependence instead of $\cos^2\theta$. The fitted curve is:
\[
I(\text{Intensity}) = 57.1 \sin^2(0.19\ i(\text{current}) + 0.03) + 0.7
\]
shows relation with the experimental data points, confirming the sinusoidal dependence of intensity. Thus, the experimental results successfully verify Malus's law within experimental uncertainty.

\section{Propagation of Error}

The Verdet constant is given by

\begin{subequations}
    \begin{align}
        V = \frac{\theta}{H L} \\
        H = \pi N I
    \end{align}
\end{subequations}

where $\theta$ is the angle of rotation,$H$ is magnetic field, $I$ is the current, $L$ is the length of the medium, and $N$ is the number of turns per unit length.

Taking the natural logarithm on both sides,

\begin{equation}
\ln V = \ln \theta - \ln I - \ln L - \ln N
\end{equation}

Differentiating, we obtain

\begin{equation}
\boxed{\frac{\Delta V}{V}
=
\frac{\Delta \theta}{\theta}
+
\frac{\Delta H}{H}
+
\frac{\Delta L}{L}}
\end{equation}

we calculate the error for a data from the table of observations.

\begin{align*}
\theta &= 240 \ \text{min}, \\
H &= 1260.6583 \ \text{Oe}, \\
V &= 0.019037673 \ \text{min}\ \text{Oe}^{-1}\text{cm}^{-1}
\end{align*}

The instrumental uncertainties are taken as:

\begin{align*}
\Delta \theta &= 0.1^\circ = 6 \ \text{min}, \\
\Delta I &= 0.01 \ \text{A}, \\
L &= 10 \ \text{cm}
\end{align*}

Since $H = \pi N I$, the relative error in $H$ is

\begin{equation}
\frac{\Delta H}{H} = \frac{\Delta I}{I} = \frac{0.01}{2.04} = 0.0049
\end{equation}

Now, substituting into the error formula (note that $L$ is constant):

\begin{equation}
\frac{\Delta V}{V}
=
\frac{6}{240}
+
0.0049 = 0.0299
\end{equation}

Hence, the maximum possible error is

\begin{equation}
\Delta V = 0.019037673 \times 0.0299
= 5.69 \times 10^{-4}
\end{equation}

Therefore, the Verdet constant for this data point is $V = (1.90 \pm 0.057)\times 10^{-2} \text{ min Oe}^{-1} \text{ cm}^{-1}$

\section{Sources of Error}

\subsection{Thermal and Environmental Effects}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Sample Heating:} The resistive heating of the electromagnet coil can transfer heat to the flint glass sample. This temperature increase can alter the Verdet constant of the glass and produce convection currents that locally vary the refractive index, introducing unwanted intensity changes.
    \item \textbf{Laser Intensity Drift:} Semiconductor diode lasers often exhibit slow temporal variations in output intensity, particularly before the laser reaches thermal equilibrium. If the laser is not allowed sufficient warm-up time, this drift can be mischaracterized as a change in transmitted intensity due to polarization rotation.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Instrumental and Alignment Limitations}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Detector Non-linearity and Spatial Sensitivity:} The photodiode signal may not respond perfectly linearly to incident light intensity. Furthermore, if the laser beam is not strictly centered on the active area of the photodiode, any slight beam deflection caused by rotating the optical components can cause spurious intensity readings.
    \item \textbf{Magnetic Field Non-uniformity:} The standard theoretical formula $\varphi = VHL$ assumes a perfectly uniform magnetic field $H$ across the entire length of the optical medium. In reality, the field lines begin to diverge near the ends of the solenoid, meaning the magnetic field is not perfectly constant across the entire length of the glass rod.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Observational Uncertainty}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Parallax Error in Angular Measurement:} The physical design of the setup, specifically the proximity of the electromagnet's cylindrical housing to the analyzing polarizer, restricts the observer's direct line of sight to the graduated scale. This obstruction forces the experimenter to read the angle from an oblique viewing position, introducing a parallax error that reduces the precision of the rotational measurements.
    \item \textbf{Resolution of the Null Point:} Accurately determining the exact angle of minimum transmitted intensity (the "darkness" point) is limited by the mechanical resolution (least count) of the precision polarizer mount. Additionally, at near-zero intensity, the signal-to-noise ratio of the photodiode drops, making the exact minimum difficult to pinpoint precisely.
    \item \textbf{Backlash Error:} Mechanical play or backlash in the rotational mount of the analyzing polarizer can introduce discrepancies if the minimum intensity angle is approached from different directions (clockwise vs. counter-clockwise). Though while taking the reading we maintain single convention (clockwise) to make the P2 for minimum intensity.
\end{itemize}
